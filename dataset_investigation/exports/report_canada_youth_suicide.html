Introduction¶

Gapminder World (link) provides a number of datasets about how people live their lives in different countries, tracked across years, and for a number of different indicators.

As a resident of Canada, I wanted to investigate what factors may contribute to suicide among Canadian youth. Gapminder provides suicide rates for youth ages 0-14.

I examined additional datasets to see if there was any correlation to suicide rates:

  • Sugar consumption
  • Government and civil society aid
  • Electricity use

These data sets, like the suicide rates data set, have several decades of data available for Canada.

Specifically, I wondered:

  1. Does increased sugar consumption increase suicide rates?
  2. Does increased government and civil society aid decrease suicide rates?
  3. Is there a correlation between consumption of electricy and suicide rates?
  4. Why has the suicide rate dipped since its peak in the 1990s?

Data Wrangling¶

General Properties¶

Each Gapminder dataset can be downloaded as an Excel file. The Excel file typically has a number of sheets, one of which contains the actual data. For each, I exported the Excel file as CSV and renamed the data sheet to indicate what data it contained. Now each can be loaded into its own dataframe.

Limitations: Gapminder provided some datasets that I thought would be interesting to compare to the suicide rates, but were not included because of limitations of that data. For example, some of the datasets did not include Canada. Others did not provide enough data. One example of the latter, regarding tooth decay, had only a single year of data, and could not be included in this report.

Data Cleaning¶

The format of the Gapminder data was consistent across data sets. Countries were represented as rows, and years as columns. Because I was concentrating on Canada, I wanted to extract the Canada row, then transpose the dataframe in order to have the years as the rows. That way, when the dataframes were combined, I could have the different datasets as the columns.

Does increased sugar consumption increase suicide rates?¶

By examining the chart below, no, there does not appear to be a correlation between suicide rates and sugar consumption. Since the turn of the 21st century, suicide rates have decreased while sugar consumption has increased.

Conclusions¶

For the data examined, I determined there was no correlation to suicides. i.e None of sugar consumption, government aid, nor electricty consumption was associated.

There are limitations regarding how this data could be interpreted. The suicide rate is national, as are all the datasets. This might hide some actual correlation if a smaller region were examined.

Further, the suicide rates are for ages 0-14, but none of the other datasets are similarly age restricted. In particular, electricity consumption is assumed to be by household, and there may be no children of the examined ages (or any children at all) in a number of those households.

Likewise, sugar consumption is not age restricted.
